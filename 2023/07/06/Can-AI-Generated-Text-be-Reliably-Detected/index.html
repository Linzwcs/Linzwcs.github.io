Improved by ChatGPT

The rapid advancement of large language models (LLMs) has ushered in a new era where machine-generated text closely resembles human-written content. However, this progress brings several significant challenges concerning the authenticity and regulation of such AI-generated text. Unscrupulous individuals exploit these generative AI tools for unethical purposes, including plagiarism, the generation of fake news, spamming, manipulating web content, and fabricating product reviews. Consequently, ensuring the responsible use of these powerful language models has become imperative. Extensive research efforts have focused on detecting AI-generated text, which can be broadly categorized into two types: binary classification approaches and zero-shot detection without additional training.

Although watermarking has been proposed as a means of detecting AI-generated text by imprinting specific patterns, its effectiveness may be limited unless all LLMs are uniformly protected. Furthermore, Krishna et al. propose an information retrieval-based detection method that involves storing the outputs of LLMs in a database. When evaluating a candidate text, the detector calculates the semantic similarity between the candidate text and the stored texts in the database.

This paper presents an empirical and theoretical analysis that highlights the challenges of developing a reliable deepfake detector for AI-generated text. The findings underscore the complexities associated with detecting AI-generated text and shed light on the limitations of current detection methods.
